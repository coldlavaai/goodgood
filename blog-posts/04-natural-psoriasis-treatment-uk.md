## Understanding Psoriasis: What's Actually Happening

**Psoriasis is an autoimmune condition** where your immune system attacks healthy skin cells, causing rapid cell turnover. Normal skin cells replace themselves every 28-30 days. With psoriasis, this happens every 3-4 days.

**The result:**
- Thick, scaly plaques
- Red, inflamed patches
- Itching and discomfort
- Sometimes painful cracking

**Types of psoriasis:**
- **Plaque psoriasis** (80-90% of cases)
- **Guttate psoriasis** (small, drop-shaped lesions)
- **Inverse psoriasis** (in skin folds)
- **Pustular psoriasis** (rare, severe)
- **Erythrodermic psoriasis** (very rare, medical emergency)

**Important:** Severe psoriasis should be managed by a dermatologist. Natural treatments work best for mild to moderate cases.

## Natural Treatments with Scientific Evidence

### 1. CBD (Cannabidiol) for Psoriasis

**The science:**
A 2020 study in *Dermatology and Therapy* found that CBD:
- Slows keratinocyte proliferation (the rapid cell growth in psoriasis)
- Reduces inflammation
- Modulates immune response
- Relieves itching

**How it works:**
CBD interacts with your skin's endocannabinoid system to regulate cell growth and inflammation - the two key processes that go wrong in psoriasis.

**What concentration you need:**
For psoriasis plaques, look for **2000mg+ CBD per 50ml jar**. Lower concentrations might help with maintenance but won't tackle active plaques.

**Real user experience:**
"I had psoriasis on my hands for 6 years. Steroids thinned my skin and it always came back. High-strength CBD balm cleared it by 90% in 2 months. First time in years I can shake hands without embarrassment." - Andy M., Manchester

**UK legality:** Completely legal when used topically (must contain <0.2% THC).

### 2. Omega-3 Fatty Acids

**The science:**
Multiple studies show that omega-3s reduce inflammation and may slow skin cell growth in psoriasis.

**Best sources:**
- **Fatty fish:** Salmon, mackerel, sardines (3-4 servings/week)
- **Fish oil supplements:** 2-3g daily EPA+DHA
- **Flaxseed oil:** Alternative for vegetarians (though less effective)

**What the evidence shows:**
A 2014 meta-analysis in the *Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology* found that omega-3 supplementation reduced psoriasis severity scores by an average of 28%.

**UK availability:**
- NHS can prescribe Omacor (omega-3) for cardiovascular reasons, sometimes helpful for psoriasis
- Over-the-counter supplements available at Holland & Barrett, Boots
- Look for high EPA content (not just generic "fish oil")

### 3. Vitamin D

**The science:**
Vitamin D plays a crucial role in regulating skin cell growth. The prescription cream Dovonex is actually a vitamin D analogue.

**Natural vitamin D:**
- **Sunlight:** 10-15 minutes daily (difficult in UK!)
- **Supplements:** 1000-4000 IU daily (UK NHS recommends 400 IU minimum)
- **Food sources:** Fatty fish, egg yolks, fortified foods

**Important:** **Don't mega-dose vitamin D** without blood tests. Too much can be harmful.

**UK-specific:** The NHS recommends vitamin D supplementation for everyone in the UK from October to March due to lack of sunlight.

### 4. Aloe Vera

**The science:**
A 2020 review in *Skinmed* found that topical aloe vera gel significantly reduced psoriasis symptoms in 70% of participants.

**How to use:**
- Pure aloe vera gel (99%+)
- Apply directly to plaques 2-3 times daily
- Look for UK brands without added fragrance or alcohol

**Why it works:**
- Anti-inflammatory compounds
- Moisturizing properties
- Promotes healing

**Realistic expectations:** Aloe won't clear severe psoriasis, but it can reduce redness and scaling in mild cases.

### 5. Apple Cider Vinegar

**The evidence:**
Mostly anecdotal, but many psoriasis patients swear by it for scalp psoriasis.

**How to use:**
- Dilute 1:1 with water
- Apply to scalp for 20 minutes
- Rinse thoroughly
- Use 2-3 times/week

**Warning:** Don't use on cracked or bleeding skin - it will burn intensely.

### 6. Turmeric (Curcumin)

**The science:**
Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, has potent anti-inflammatory properties.

**Evidence:**
A 2018 study found that curcumin gel reduced psoriasis plaques better than placebo.

**How to use:**
- **Topical:** Turmeric paste or curcumin cream
- **Oral:** 1-3g curcumin supplements daily (with black pepper for absorption)
- **Dietary:** Add turmeric to cooking

**UK availability:**
Curcumin supplements widely available at health food stores and pharmacies.

### 7. Dead Sea Salt Baths

**The science:**
The Dead Sea is famous for psoriasis treatment. The high mineral content (magnesium, bromide, potassium) has therapeutic effects.

**How to use:**
- Add 1-2 cups Dead Sea salt to warm bath
- Soak for 15-20 minutes
- 2-3 times weekly

**UK products:**
Dead Sea salts available at Boots, Holland & Barrett, or online.

**Bonus:** The NHS sometimes funds trips to the Dead Sea for severe psoriasis patients (rare, but possible through dermatology referral).

## Dietary Approaches for Psoriasis

**Disclaimer:** Diet alone won't cure psoriasis, but it can significantly reduce inflammation.

### The Anti-Inflammatory Diet

**Foods to increase:**
- **Fatty fish** (omega-3s)
- **Leafy greens** (antioxidants)
- **Berries** (anti-inflammatory compounds)
- **Nuts and seeds** (healthy fats)
- **Olive oil** (oleocanthal - natural anti-inflammatory)
- **Turmeric and ginger** (curcumin and gingerol)

**Foods to reduce or eliminate:**
- **Refined sugar** (promotes inflammation)
- **Red meat** (high in arachidonic acid - inflammatory)
- **Dairy** (trigger for some people)
- **Alcohol** (especially beer - linked to psoriasis flares)
- **Processed foods** (additives and inflammatory oils)
- **Nightshades** (some people react to tomatoes, peppers, potatoes)

**UK-specific:**
- The NHS doesn't officially recommend dietary changes for psoriasis
- But the British Association of Dermatologists acknowledges some patients benefit

### Gluten-Free Diet

**The evidence:**
People with celiac disease have higher rates of psoriasis. Some (not all) psoriasis patients improve on gluten-free diets.

**Should you try it?**
- Get tested for celiac disease first (blood test available on NHS)
- If negative but you suspect gluten sensitivity, try eliminating for 3 months
- Reintroduce to see if psoriasis worsens

**UK resources:**
Coeliac UK provides excellent resources for gluten-free living.

## Lifestyle Modifications That Help

### 1. Stress Management

**The connection:**
Stress is a **proven psoriasis trigger**. It activates inflammatory pathways and can cause flares.

**Evidence-based stress reduction:**
- **Mindfulness meditation:** Shown to reduce psoriasis severity by 25% in studies
- **Yoga:** Reduces stress hormones, improves sleep
- **Deep breathing:** Activates parasympathetic nervous system
- **Therapy:** CBT particularly effective

**UK resources:**
- NHS Talking Therapies (free)
- Headspace/Calm apps (meditation)
- British Association for Counselling and Psychotherapy

### 2. Sleep Optimization

**The link:**
Poor sleep worsens inflammation. Psoriasis can cause poor sleep (itching). It's a vicious cycle.

**How to break it:**
- Keep bedroom cool (16-18Â°C)
- Cotton sheets (breathable)
- Moisturize before bed
- Antihistamine if needed (ask GP)
- No screens 1 hour before sleep

### 3. Avoid Triggers

**Common psoriasis triggers:**
- **Skin injury** (Koebner phenomenon - psoriasis appears at injury sites)
- **Infections** (strep throat often triggers guttate psoriasis)
- **Medications** (beta-blockers, lithium, antimalarials)
- **Weather** (cold, dry weather worsens it for most people)
- **Smoking** (doubles your psoriasis risk)
- **Alcohol** (especially beer)

**Practical UK tips:**
- Winter is harsh on psoriasis - use humidifiers
- Hot showers strip oils - keep them lukewarm
- Moisturize immediately after bathing

## What About UV Light Therapy?

**Phototherapy (UV light treatment)** is one of the most effective psoriasis treatments.

**Available on NHS:**
- Narrowband UVB (most common)
- PUVA (UVA + psoralen drug)
- Targeted phototherapy (for small areas)

**Natural alternative:**
Controlled sun exposure (10-15 minutes daily in summer). Free, effective, but:
- Hard to get in UK (not enough sun!)
- Risk of sunburn and skin cancer if overdone
- Need to be careful with timing

**Bottom line:** If you have moderate to severe psoriasis, ask your GP for a phototherapy referral. It's free on the NHS and highly effective.

## When to See a Dermatologist

Natural treatments work well for mild psoriasis. But you should see a dermatologist if:

- **Psoriasis covers >10% of your body**
- **It's significantly affecting your quality of life**
- **You have psoriatic arthritis** (joint pain with psoriasis)
- **Natural treatments haven't helped after 3 months**
- **You're experiencing depression or anxiety** related to psoriasis

**Getting a referral:**
Ask your GP. Wait times vary (2-12 weeks typically in UK).

**What to expect:**
Dermatologist can offer:
- Stronger topical treatments
- Phototherapy
- Systemic medications (methotrexate, biologics)
- Specialist advice

## Realistic Expectations

- Reduce inflammation and scaling

## FAQs

**Can psoriasis be cured naturally?**
No. Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune condition with no cure (natural or medical). But it can be managed so effectively that you're symptom-free for long periods.

**How long do natural treatments take to work?**
Most people see initial improvement within 2-4 weeks, with maximum benefit at 8-12 weeks. Natural treatments are slower than steroids but provide longer-lasting results.

**Is CBD better than steroid cream for psoriasis?**
CBD works differently - it's anti-inflammatory without the side effects of steroids (skin thinning, dependency). For mild to moderate psoriasis, many people find high-strength CBD as effective as steroids without the risks.

**Can diet really affect psoriasis?**
Yes. While diet alone won't cure psoriasis, reducing inflammatory foods can significantly reduce flare-ups for many people. Worth trying for 3 months.

**Should I stop my prescription medications?**
Not without your doctor's guidance. Natural treatments can complement or eventually replace prescriptions, but do this gradually and under medical supervision.

## What Makes an Eczema Cream Actually Effective?

Before we dive into specific products, let's understand what separates effective eczema creams from expensive moisturizers with fancy packaging.

**Essential criteria:**

1. **Anti-inflammatory ingredients** (not just moisturizers)
2. **Barrier repair components** (ceramides, fatty acids)
3. **High concentration of actives** (not token amounts)
4. **No common irritants** (fragrance, parabens, sulfates)
5. **Appropriate pH** (slightly acidic, like healthy skin)

**Why most products fail:**
They're glorified moisturizers. They add temporary hydration but don't address the inflammation or barrier dysfunction that cause eczema.

## Prescription vs. Over-the-Counter vs. Natural

Let's be clear about what each category can and can't do:

### Prescription Steroid Creams

**Examples:** Hydrocortisone, betamethasone (Betnovate), clobetasol (Dermovate)

**Pros:**
- Work fast (days)
- Very effective for acute flares
- Free on NHS

**Cons:**
- Thin skin with long-term use
- Create dependency
- Rebound flares when stopped
- Can't use indefinitely

**Best for:** Severe flare-ups under medical supervision

### Over-the-Counter Emollients

**Examples:** E45, Aveeno, CeraVe, Diprobase

**Pros:**
- Safe for daily use
- Affordable
- Available everywhere
- No prescription needed

**Cons:**
- Only moisturize, don't treat inflammation
- Need frequent reapplication
- Limited effectiveness for moderate-severe eczema

**Best for:** Maintenance between flares, mild dryness

### Natural Anti-Inflammatory Treatments

**Examples:** High-strength CBD balms, colloidal oatmeal, calendula

**Pros:**
- Treat inflammation without steroids
- Safe for long-term use
- No skin thinning
- Address root causes

**Cons:**
- More expensive initially
- Take longer to work (weeks, not days)
- Quality varies dramatically between brands

**Best for:** Long-term management, moderate eczema, reducing steroid dependence

## Ingredients to AVOID

These can trigger or worsen eczema:

**Common irritants:**
- Fragrance (synthetic or natural essential oils)
- Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS)
- Alcohol (especially denatured alcohol)
- Parabens (for sensitive skin)
- Coconut oil (comedogenic, can worsen some eczema)

**Controversial ingredients:**
- Petroleum jelly - occlusive but doesn't repair barrier
- Mineral oil - same as above
- Lanolin - can cause allergic reactions in some people

## The Application Method Matters

Even the best cream won't work if you apply it wrong:

**Best practices:**

1. **Apply to damp skin** - Within 3 minutes of bathing
2. **Use enough** - Adults need 500g/week for whole body
3. **Pat, don't rub** - Reduces irritation
4. **Downward strokes** - Follows hair growth, prevents folliculitis
5. **Frequency** - At least twice daily, more if needed

**Common mistakes:**
- Applying to dry skin (50% less absorption)
- Using too little (most people use 25% of recommended amount)
- Rubbing vigorously (can irritate)
- Inconsistent application (need daily routine)

## When to See Your GP

Over-the-counter and natural treatments work for many people, but see your GP if:

- Eczema isn't improving after 4 weeks of consistent treatment
- Signs of infection (yellow crusts, oozing, fever)
- Eczema covering >10% of body
- Severely impacting sleep or daily life
- Depression or anxiety related to skin condition

**What your GP can offer:**
- Prescription emollients (unlimited, free)
- Steroid creams for flares
- Antibiotic cream/tablets if infected
- Dermatology referral if needed
- Phototherapy (UV light treatment)

## FAQs

**What's the best cream for baby eczema in the UK?**
Start with simple emollients (Aveeno Baby, Childs Farm) and calendula cream. If not effective, ask health visitor about prescribed emollients. CBD is safe from 6 months but consult pediatrician first.

**Can I get eczema cream on NHS prescription?**
Yes - all emollients are free on NHS prescription. Some barrier repair creams also available. Steroid creams require GP consultation.

**How long does it take for natural eczema creams to work?**
Most natural treatments take 2-4 weeks for noticeable improvement, 8-12 weeks for maximum benefit. Steroids work in days but have long-term risks.

**Should I use cream or ointment for eczema?**
Ointments are thicker and more occlusive - better for very dry skin and nighttime. Creams absorb faster - better for daytime. Both work; it's personal preference.

**Can I use eczema cream on my face?**
Not all products are suitable for facial use. Look for gentle, non-comedogenic formulations specifically labeled for face use.
